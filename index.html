What Goes Around Comes Around

The evolving role of the secondhand clothing market in San Francisco

By Sophie D'Amato, Kevin Kaspar, Agnes Lee and Jess Chen

Amidst the beginning of the pandemic in early 2020, work from home allowed time to work on the home. As many looked toward decluttering and minimizing areas like their closet space, the pandemic served as yet another boost in overall recognition for the secondhand clothing market, especially for and by younger shoppers.

Most notably, ecommerce platforms like Depop and Ebay, grew enormously. Founded in London in 2011, Depop saw a culture shift in 2020. Activity spiked at the start of lockdowns with many of its highest-earning sellers opening shops during the pandemic, doubling revenue and gross merchandise sales to $70 million and $650 million, respectively. Depop's success hasn't slowed since — its app saw an 106 percent increase of monthly active users in only four months, and the company was recently acquired by Etsy for $1.6 billion in 2021.

According to company data, Depop's main market is Gen-Z, with 90 percent of its buyers and sellers aged 25 or younger. That comes at no surprise based on the cultural moments shaped by current media, most recently (and influentially) HBO's Euphoria that dons its characters in the Y2K aesthetic with a desirable wardrobe that is often thrifted or vintage.

Transforming from verb to culture, the term "thrifting" has now come to commonly refer to the community around the secondhand clothing market, and the city of San Francisco is no exception. Known for its eclectic culture, San Francisco has been a hotspot for secondhand clothing for decades.

The Essentials: Thrift Terms to Understand

While these traditional thrifting chains are still thriving, the popularity of buying secondhand has transformed to more common and stylish. This market expansion has led to an abundance of curated vintage storefronts across the city, and especially in the Haight-Ashbury neighborhood of San Francisco.

"Thrifting" is now used to describe shopping at these curated spots, too, but the pre-owned clothing in curated stores goes for much higher prices than clothing at traditional thrift stores, and often even more than new clothing.

One subcategory of curated secondhand clothing, "vintage" refers mainly to the age of (and the implication of scarcity from) the clothing, although Independent Vintage Appraiser Deborah Miller sees flexibility with this definition.

"'Thrifted' denotes your buying on a different level of the market, and vintage has to do with the age. So what is vintage? What used to be vintage? 100 years [ago]? No, that's 'antique.' Is [vintage] 'Mean Girls' [of 2004]? I would say absolutely," Miller said.

Miller added, "I would say maybe anything over 10 years. Different people have different ideas of vintage, depending on what they feel is currently in style. Something five years old might be outdated. But if it's 10 years old, it might be considered vintage because it just has to do with the aesthetic."

"Different people have different ideas of vintage, depending on what they feel is currently in style."
- Deborah Miller

A "curated" shop intentionally hand-picks pieces to denote the store's style and aesthetic. For example, Indigo Vintage on Haight Street runs as a cooperative for curated clothing.

"Each rack in the store is its own vendor and they do all their own curating. Then, we sell their clothing on consignment, so there's like 30 different small businesses in the space," Assistant Manager Caitlyn Ryan said. "I [also] do some curating for the shop, and I like to do it more based off of what I like and what I want to see people wearing."

Wasteland, on the other hand, sticks to designer vintage. According to Inventory Manager Taylor Burnside, the store accepts donations by "what brands are in and what brands are out" while still buying for modern and designer vintage: “When I started [working], we would take this brand, Billionaire Boys Club, which is like a streetwear brand. But like now, that's done we don't take The Club or Thrasher, [when] that was the whole thing. Things just change fast, and you have to be in the know of that."

Secondhand Trends Continue to Skyrocket

Thrifters say their reasons for shopping secondhand range from expression of personal style, to sustainability to affordability.

Hannah Morley, a regular at the Haight Street Goodwill, started thrifting at a young age with her family.

"My parents were lower middle class, so they took [me and my brother] to thrift stores when we were really young," Morley said.

"I had an awakening into my own style and just started shopping at thrift stores," Morley said. "It's just about wearing clothes that express how you feel inside and express your values. My clothing tends to be very colorful, very bright, and hopefully interesting."

"It's just about wearing clothes that express how you feel inside and express your values."
- Hannah Morley

Morley said, “What I want when I walk down the street is to make other people smile when they look at me because that's a reflection of my values. I want to exude love into the world, and so that is what I choose to put on my body and it means a lot to me.”

Despite her understanding of the markets and life cycles for clothing, Miller also understands her 17-year-old and 19-year-old daughters' relationship to the clothing they purchase.

"I do think my daughters now are really careful about buying fast fashion," Miller said, noting they get great, lightly worn pieces secondhand. "It's an aspect of just really wanting to exert your personality more in a sea of sameness. It's a great way of exerting your individual sartorial."

Ryan also addressed environmental considerations with secondhand shopping. She noted that "slower fashion" is a substantial part of the secondhand trend: "A lot of clothing items end up in a landfill before they've even been worn."

Vintage production quality and attention to detail are also reasons to shop at curated secondhand stores.

Compared to newer textiles from the same brands, like Forever 21, pieces from the 90's have held up well, Ryan said. "As production numbers have gone up in fast fashion, the quality has definitely gone down. Not only is there so much overproduction, it's also not sustainable at all. The vintage clothing we get — even though it's like 30 years older — will last way longer than anything they're making nowadays.”

The Ethics of Today's Thrift Stores

The pandemic made way for the online secondhand market to expand as brick-and-mortar thrift shops struggled.

But Morley believes that "thrift stores, as they always have been, are a really great equalizer [since] it really doesn't matter how much income you have or where you come from."

In addition, secondhand products offer one-of-a-kind options that are sometimes even more authentic than the fast fashion trends that mimic them. According to Miller, "certain things at different eras appeal to different age groups,” just as current “young people care probably less about the label itself" unlike the label craze in the 1980s.

A slow-fashion idealist, Morley uses secondhand shopping as a way to set examples of how to create a sustainable cycle of consumption while also giving back to secondhand stores in order to do her part in the system: "I think that any time that you shop secondhand, it's giving back. It is sustainable, in and of itself. I shop a lot at thrift stores, which means I purchase a lot. But I also try to cycle through my closet pretty often and give away clothes as well to keep the cycle going."

And to the question of scarcity, additional expansion to secondhand's customer base has not made a huge impact on inventory numbers. In fact, the fashion industry as a whole struggles with having way too many clothes.

"Zara, [for example], there's a new collection every five weeks. So, you get a backlog of a lot of stuff, and it's put out in the secondary market really quickly and may only be like five or seven months old. But it's not really out of fashion; it's just no longer the newest thing," Miller said.

While the current trend of "thrifting" may not be emptying racks at secondhand stores, this market, once centered on supporting low-income communities, has expanded to the upper-middle class and become more profitable due to the revenue incentive of rising prices to match the excessive demand.

What's Next for the Thrifting Community?

One thing's expected: Thrifting is not going anywhere. From the community of fashion and vintage lovers it fosters all the way to the freedom of expression it encourages, the question is more about what's next for this ever-growing market.

Although "thrifting" is an individualistic, sustainable and financially-friendly way for almost anyone to express their style and values, it does come at a cost. While there really is no scarcity of the clothing itself, both retail and secondhand, constant consumption has created a perpetual competitive market. As the demand for their products increase, the stores that once only supplied those in need are now overridden with teens and millennials looking for an "authentic" take on re-emerging fashion cycles.

“[Her] ideal is that people are purchasing from thrift stores, but they're also giving back to [them]."
- Hannah Morley

Buying secondhand may have less consequential effects on the environment than fast-fashion, but it is still sometimes rooted in overconsumption. Morley says "her ideal is that people are purchasing from thrift stores, but they're also giving back to [them]” as a way to take the clothing cycle "out from the global ecosystem and make it very grassroots and community-based."
